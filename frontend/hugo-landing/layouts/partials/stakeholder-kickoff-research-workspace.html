Product & Business Context — suggested questions

What is Aria and what does it do? + Add a Question for the Kick-off Call

What is Aria?

Aria is an AI agent platform built natively into Lumina, used for enterprise workflow automation.

Core capabilities: It connects to a company’s existing tools — CRMs, ERPs, ticketing systems, communication platforms — and autonomously executes multi-step processes that currently require manual coordination across teams.

Unlike a chatbot that answers questions or a co-pilot that makes suggestions, Aria takes action. It can qualify leads and update CRM records, route and resolve support tickets, trigger approval workflows, synthesise data from multiple systems and produce reports, and escalate edge cases to the right human at the right time.

AI agentWorkflow automationEnterprise

What is the business goal behind this training?

Lumina’s go-to-market strategy for Aria depends almost entirely on the sales team’s ability to demonstrate its value in live customer conversations.

The business goal is straightforward: close more enterprise deals, faster. The training problem underneath it is harder: new reps arrive with limited AI fluency, struggle to explain what an “AI agent” actually does without sounding vague, and lose deals early when they can’t respond confidently to objections about data security, implementation complexity, or ROI.

What has already been tried to solve this problem?

Three prior attempts, all with limited success:

1. Product wiki + self-study. A comprehensive Notion site was built out with Aria’s architecture, use cases, and FAQs. Adoption was low: reps reported it felt like “reading a technical manual before knowing what the job actually is.” Information without context doesn’t stick.

2. Manager-led onboarding sessions. Weekly group calls where sales managers walked through product positioning. Feedback was mixed. The sessions were useful but inconsistent across teams: quality depended heavily on the manager. No structured practice, no assessment of whether reps could actually use what they heard.

3. Role-play with a buddy rep. Informal peer shadowing programme. High satisfaction ratings but zero standardisation. New reps absorbed whichever habits — good or bad — their buddy happened to have.

Audience & Learner Analysis — suggested questions

Who are the sales reps we’re designing for?

The primary audience is newly hired Account Executives at Lumina, globally distributed, within their first 90 days. The cohort is heterogeneous in almost every dimension that matters for training design.

Experience range: roughly 40% are experienced enterprise sellers from other SaaS companies; 35% are mid-career with some B2B sales experience but new to enterprise; 25% are early-career or career-changers with limited sales background. This means the same training content needs to be non-patronising for veterans while still being sufficient for novices.

AI fluency ranges from confident to near-zero. About 15% use AI tools daily. About 30% have experimented occasionally. The remaining 55% have surface-level familiarity at best — they know what ChatGPT is but have never thought critically about what an “AI agent” does versus a “co-pilot” or a “chatbot.”

Heterogeneous cohortAI fluency gapGlobal

What are the reps’ biggest challenges right now?

Three challenges surface consistently in manager feedback and rep exit interviews from failed deals:

Can’t explain Aria in plain language. When asked “what does Aria actually do?”, reps default to technical language (“autonomous multi-step workflow execution”) that means nothing to a VP of Operations who just wants to know if it’ll replace their team. The inability to translate product into prospect language is the single most cited reason for early deal loss.

Under-prepared for the “we tried automation before” objection. A significant portion of enterprise prospects have had bad experiences with automation tools. Reps who can’t acknowledge that history and differentiate Aria from it lose credibility fast.

Unclear on use case fit. Reps know Aria’s core capabilities but struggle to map those capabilities to a specific prospect’s workflow. Discovery calls stay surface-level because reps don’t know what questions to ask to uncover a strong use case match.

The 31% figure understates the complexity. Within that group, English proficiency ranges from near-native to functional but effortful. For those in the latter group, content that’s dense, jargon-heavy, or delivered at speed in video format will have a disproportionate cognitive cost.

Design implications: plain language standard (aim for Grade 8 readability in English), closed captions on all video content, no content that relies solely on audio cues, and scenario language that avoids idioms that don’t translate across cultures.

Secondary consideration: the cultural assumptions baked into sales training (directness, assertiveness, linear negotiation) don’t map cleanly across all markets. Scenario design should account for this.

How do reps currently learn new products?

No single dominant channel: learning happens through a patchwork of formal and informal sources, and reps are largely self-directing which they use and when.

Formal: A 2-day onboarding session in week 1 (mostly product overview + HR logistics), access to a product wiki, and a quarterly all-hands where product updates are shared. None of these are assessed; there’s no verification that learning occurred.

Informal: Asking a more experienced colleague (most common), watching recordings of other reps’ customer calls (where available), and Slack channels like #aria-questions and #wins where reps share what’s working. This informal layer is where most actionable knowledge actually transfers — but it’s invisible, inconsistent, and dependent on who you sit near.

On-the-job: Trial and error in live customer calls. This is universally cited as the most effective learning method — and also the highest-stakes. Failed deals are expensive teachers.

Stakeholders & Decision-Makers — suggested questions

Who are the key stakeholders on this project?

Four stakeholder groups, each with different leverage and different definitions of success:

Executive sponsor: VP of Sales. Cares about quota attainment. Will approve scope and budget but will disengage if led with learning objectives instead of revenue outcomes. Frame everything in terms of ramp time and deal conversion.

Client / day-to-day sponsor: Jordan Kim, Sales Manager. Your primary relationship and the person who owns the sales team’s performance. She’ll push for training that translates directly into rep behaviour in the field — fast. She controls access to the team, shapes what gets prioritised, and will be your most visible advocate or critic depending on how the project goes.

SME / Product Lead: Dr. Priya Nair. The deepest Aria product expert you’ll work with. She controls the accuracy gate on all technical content and use case framing. Her buy-in is essential — if the product details are wrong or the use cases feel contrived, she’ll catch it and the training loses credibility with reps who know the product.

End learners as stakeholders: Senior reps in pilot testing. Their feedback shapes whether the content feels relevant or patronising. Don’t design without them in the room at some point.

Who has final approval authority?

Approval flows through two parallel tracks that need to converge before anything launches:

Business approval: Jordan Kim as Sales Manager holds day-to-day sign-off on direction, scope, and timeline. For budget decisions above a certain threshold, she escalates to the VP of Sales — but in practice, she’ll bring a recommendation, not a question. Your job is to give Jordan something she can defend upward.

Product accuracy approval: Dr. Priya Nair holds the quality gate on all content that describes how Aria works — use cases, capability claims, integration details, demo scenarios. Nothing that touches product accuracy ships without her sign-off. This is non-negotiable and has precedent: she has blocked content before.

The dynamic to manage: Jordan optimises for speed and field applicability; Priya optimises for technical accuracy. When a scenario feels real to Jordan but overstates what Aria can do, Priya will flag it. That’s a healthy tension — but only if you catch the conflict at the design stage, not during stakeholder review of a finished build.

What is the budget and what does it cover?

Budget has not been confirmed in writing — this is a key item to resolve in the kickoff call. What’s known informally:

Jordan has verbal approval for “a reasonable enablement spend” from the VP of Sales. Based on comparable projects at Lumina, that likely means $30k–$55k for a 60-minute eLearning build. However, “reasonable” is doing a lot of work in that sentence and hasn’t been tested against a detailed scope.

Known inclusions: eLearning development, LMS configuration, and a manager communication package. Known exclusions (almost certainly): video production with external actors, custom illustration, or translated versions beyond English and Spanish in the first release.

Design implication: Don’t design to a budget you don’t have confirmed. The kickoff call must produce a confirmed number or a clear escalation path. Designing a proposal that assumes $55k and presenting it to a client thinking $30k is recoverable — but only if you catch it early.

What tools and technology are available?

LMS: Lumina uses Workday Learning. SCORM 1.2 compliance is required for tracking. Verify authoring tool output against Workday’s known quirks before final build — mobile rendering and SCORM completion triggers are common failure points.

Authoring tools: Articulate Storyline 360 and Rise 360 (both licensed), Camtasia for screen-capture video. No Lectora, no custom HTML builds unless explicitly approved.

Video: No production budget for live-action video. AI-generated avatars (Synthesia or similar) are an open question — Jordan is interested in the efficiency gain, Priya wants to review any AI-generated representation of how Aria works before it goes to reps. Resolve this at the design stage.

Collaboration: Stakeholder reviews via SharePoint shared documents. No Figma access for stakeholders — wireframes need to be in a format they can comment on without a tool login.

What does ‘done’ look like to each stakeholder?

This is the most important question in the constraints category — and the one most often left unasked until it’s too late.

Jordan’s definition (Sales Manager): Her team is closing deals faster. Specifically: time-to-first-conversation drops below 30 days for the next cohort, and her managers stop fielding the same “how do I explain what Aria does?” question from new reps. She’ll track completion rates in Workday and watch conversion metrics in the next 90-day window. If those numbers don’t move, the training didn’t work — regardless of what the design looked like.

Priya’s definition (SME / Product Lead): Every claim made in the training about Aria’s capabilities is accurate, no scenario overstates what the product can do, and reps come out able to describe Aria’s technical differentiators without misrepresenting them to a sophisticated buyer. She’ll spot-check by listening to calls from trained reps. A rep who confidently says something incorrect about Aria in a customer call is a training failure in her book.

The tension: Jordan measures success in pipeline outcomes. Priya measures it in product accuracy. A training that scores high on one can still fail the other. Design for both simultaneously — scenario content that is technically accurate and field-applicable is the only version that satisfies this room.
